Title + Subtitle
Riding the Dark Twisties

Doing the thing slower still counts as doing the thing.
Full Article (paste-ready)
I rode at night for the first time this week. Where I live there are no streetlights. The roads are twisties — fun in the daylight, a black tunnel once the sun goes down. I rode slower than I normally do. And I did it. That's the whole story. It's also the whole lesson. Here's what nobody tells you about learning something hard as an adult: you don't get the daylight version forever. You learn the cigar, the close, the route, the spreadsheet in good conditions — and then real life hands you the night version. Same road. No light. Same skills, suddenly with the volume turned all the way up. Most people freeze there. They tell themselves they'll go when it's brighter. They wait for the perfect conditions that never show up. I get it — the twisties at midnight are genuinely scary the first time. Your headlight only shows you the next forty feet. You can't see the whole curve. You have to trust that the road keeps going the way the last forty feet told you it would. That's operating. That's literally the job. I run three brands. I close deals in two countries. I'm building a television network. I do not see the whole road on any of it. Nobody handed me the full map of KSM TV. Nobody gave me the entire Pocono season before it started. I get forty feet of headlight at a time and I keep the bike moving. The mistake new riders make — and new operators make — is thinking the answer to fear is speed. Gun it, get it over with, prove you're not scared. Wrong. The answer to the dark is slowing down enough to actually read what the headlight is showing you, and trusting your reps. I put in the daylight hours. My hands know the bike. My body knows the lean. At night I'm not relying on my eyes — I'm relying on everything I drilled when it was easy. You build the skill in good light so you can use it in the dark. My husband rode beside me the whole way. I want to be honest about that, because the lone-wolf operator thing is a lie people sell on the internet. I didn't do the night ride alone and I'm not embarrassed by it. The strongest operators I know all have someone riding beside them — a partner, a mentor, a team that knows the road too. Independence isn't doing everything by yourself. It's choosing who's in your blind spot. So here's the operator takeaway, and it's the one I keep relearning: slower still counts. A deal that closes in ninety days still closes. A brand that takes two years to find its voice still found it. A network built one licensing meeting at a time still gets built. The market doesn't hand you a medal for speed if you wrap the bike around a tree at mile two. Read the road you can see. Trust the reps you already put in. Keep someone good in your blind spot. And keep the bike moving — even at midnight, even on the twisties, even when the headlight only gives you forty feet. I'll get faster in the dark. Everybody does. But the win this week wasn't speed. The win was that I went. That's the whole operator playbook in one sentence: when conditions get worse, the people who win are the ones who slow down instead of stopping. See you on the road. Stay in your lane. Literally. — Alexa
