Title + Subtitle
The Man Who Wanted Illegal Cigars (And What That Taught Me About Running a Business)

When your customers want something you literally cannot give them — and what you do next.
Full Article (paste-ready)
He walked in confident. Like he already knew what he wanted. I welcomed him, pointed toward the humidor. He walked the perimeter with the energy of a man on a very specific mission. "Do you have real Cubans?" he asked. I pointed to the Cohiba Blue Label. The Romeo and Julieta. Good cigars. Dominican made. Premium product. He shook his head. "No. Real Cubans." Here's where I had a choice. I could have smiled awkwardly and said "unfortunately not." I could have gotten defensive. I could have launched into a ten-minute explanation of American trade law. I chose the third option. Not because it was the smartest, but because it was honest. "Sir, Cuban cigars cannot be sold commercially in the United States. What you're asking for is not something I can legally provide." He looked at me like I had five heads. Five heads, a briefcase, and a PhD in disappointment. Apparently, he only smokes "real" Cubans. And he said it with the conviction of a man who has never read the Trading with the Enemy Act of 1917. This is the operator life. Not every customer knows what's possible. Not every patron knows the rules. And not every person who walks through your door is ready to hear the answer to the question they're actually asking. Here's what I've learned after running Nell's Social: Your job is not to give people exactly what they want. Your job is to give them the best version of what's actually available — and make them glad they chose it. That man wanted an illegal Cuban. I cannot give him that. What I can give him is a world-class Dominican cigar from one of the finest tobacco-producing regions on the planet. Cohiba Blue Label is not a consolation prize. It's a premium product with a story, a flavor profile, and a legacy. The real lesson isn't about cigars. It's about managing expectations while holding your ground. I didn't apologize for not carrying something I legally cannot carry. I didn't pretend the rules don't exist to make a sale. And I didn't collapse under the weight of his disappointment. I explained. Calmly. With conviction. And then I suggested something excellent. That's the operator move. Not capitulation. Not defensiveness. Just clarity — and a redirect to something better. He bought the Cohiba Blue Label. He didn't look happy about it. But he bought it. And that, my friends, is a Tuesday at Nell's Social. There's no real free time in this life. I've stopped expecting it. Instead, I make time — and lately that looks like cleaning my house late at night with music on, riding my motorcycle on a Sunday morning, and sitting with a good cigar before the week picks back up. The operator life isn't for everyone. But for those of us who chose it, the chaos is the point. See you next week. — Alexa
