Title + Subtitle
I Dumped the Bike. I Passed Anyway.

What a failed MSF exam taught me about the difference between competence and performance.
Full Article (paste-ready)
I rode for two full days without stalling once. Not one time. I hit every skill station. My braking was smooth. My low-speed control was clean. My instructor watched me work through every drill and didn't have to correct me after the first hour. By Sunday morning I was the person other students were watching to see how it's done. Then came the timed assessment. I dumped the bike. Not from a skill gap. Not because I forgot how to ride. I dumped it because the word "assessment" hit my nervous system like a brick and suddenly my hands stopped trusting what two days of muscle memory had built. That's an automatic fail. Doesn't matter what came before it. One dump, game over. The instructor was genuinely surprised. He'd watched me ride for hours. He knew it wasn't about ability. And here's what I kept thinking about on the drive home: this is not a motorcycle story. --- This is what happens when your execution environment changes. In business — in any high-stakes moment — there's a version of you that works well under normal conditions, and there's a version of you that shows up when someone's watching, when the clock is running, when the stakes feel real. Those are not automatically the same person. I have seen this in real estate. I have seen buyers who know exactly what they want, who have done the research, who are clearly ready — freeze at the negotiating table. I have seen sellers who are completely logical about their home's value in a casual conversation go emotionally irrational the moment an offer hits the table. The knowledge didn't leave. The environment changed. The MSF course doesn't just test riding. It tests whether you can access your competence under pressure. And I couldn't. Not that day. --- What I didn't do: quit. I rode around town later that same afternoon. Parking lot practice in the evening. Got back on the bike not because I needed to prove anything — because I needed to show my nervous system that dumping wasn't the end of the story. The skill is still there. The exam will happen again. Here's the operator lesson I'm sitting with this week: Failing an assessment is not the same as not being ready. It means your performance environment is not yet aligned with your competence level. That's a different problem — and it has a different solution. If you fail a test, the answer isn't always more studying. Sometimes it's more reps in conditions that simulate pressure. More time in environments where the stakes feel real so they don't paralyze you when they actually are. I know how to ride. I've been proving it for months. What I need now is practice performing under conditions that feel like assessment. In business terms: I need to put myself in more rooms where I could lose something. --- Running three brands simultaneously — the lounge, the real estate practice, the media company — means I'm always juggling multiple competence-versus-performance gaps. There are things I know how to do that I haven't performed under pressure yet. There are deals I haven't closed, pitches I haven't made, rooms I haven't walked into. The MSF fail reminded me that the gap between knowing and doing under pressure is real, it's trainable, and the only way to close it is to get back on the bike. Literally and otherwise. --- I'm retaking the exam. I'm not changing how I ride. I'm just going to log more hours in the parking lot with the pressure turned up. Because the version of me that can ride and the version of me that can perform it when someone's watching with a clipboard — those two have to meet. This week, they didn't. Next time, they will. — Alexa
