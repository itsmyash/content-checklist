Title + Subtitle
The Closing That Changed How I Show Up

I've worked with buyers in every situation. Last week I realized I hadn't worked with all of them.
Full Article (paste-ready)
Last week I closed a condo in a 55-plus community. Small deal on paper. But this one stuck with me. My buyer is visually impaired. Functionally blind. And what I assumed would be a standard closing process turned into a three-week education I did not expect. Here's what I mean: the mortgage process in 2026 is almost entirely digital. Hyperlinks. PDFs that require e-signatures. Portals that load forms on click. For most buyers, it's ten minutes of clicking. For my client, it was three hours per document. Screen readers don't parse embedded hyperlinks the way sighted users experience them. Tabbing through fields in a digital form is not intuitive when the form wasn't built for anyone other than a sighted person. The industry built the fastest closing pipeline in history. And it's completely inaccessible to a meaningful portion of the population. I sat with that for a few days. I have a child with Down syndrome. I've spent years volunteering with Special Olympics. I thought that gave me some fluency in accessibility conversations. And then I realized I had never once advocated for a visually impaired person navigating a real estate transaction. Different experience. Different gaps. I assumed my awareness translated. It didn't. Here's what I did: I printed documents. I walked him through every signature on the phone, reading field by field. I coordinated directly with the lender to get accessible formats where possible. I added two hours to every step I would normally knock out in twenty minutes. And we closed. But here's what I should have done sooner: asked, at the first consultation, what accommodations would make this process easier. Not waited until we hit a wall. That's the operator lesson this week. Not just in real estate — in everything we build. We build systems for the average user. The average client. The path of least friction for the majority. And in doing that, we accidentally wall off the people for whom friction is the default. If you run a lounge, when's the last time you thought about whether your menu is accessible to someone who's blind? If you sell real estate, when's the last time you asked a buyer what they need before assuming your process works for them? The best operators don't just close deals. They close the gap between who the system was built for and who's actually standing in front of them. I have a lot to learn here. This closing reminded me of that. And I'm grateful my buyer trusted me to figure it out alongside him. Be direct. Ask what people need. Then build accordingly. — Alexa

We have lighting that says "stay a while." But none of that would have produced Friday night. Friday night came from the space having just enough soul that when someone leaned in, the room leaned back. That's the product. Not the room. The permission inside the room.
